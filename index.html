Gross Domestic Product and Unemployment (2019)

According to the principle established by Okun's Law (Okun, 1962), there is a corresponding two percent increase in employment for every established one percent increase in GDP. The principle states that GDP levels are driven by the principles of demand and supply, and as such, an increase in demand leads to an increase in GDP. Such an increase in demand must be accompanied by a corresponding increase in productivity and employment to keep up with the demand.

With that, we will now explore more on the relationship between GDP and Unemployment Rate.

GDP and unemployment rates are linked in the sense that both are macroeconomic factors that are used to gauge the state of an economy. (Esther, 2022) A rise in the GDP is significant in the study of macroeconomic trends in a nation. This is also true of a rise or decrease in unemployment levels. GDP and unemployment rates usually go together because a decrease in the GDP is reflected in a decrease in the rate of employment.

This sums up the relationship between GDP per Capita and Unemployment Rate of a country.

The Rise of Covid-19 Event

Unemployment Rate in the world averaged 7.91% from 2011 until 2021, reaching an all time high of 8.31 percent in the year of 2020 and a record low of 7.11 percent in the year of 2019.

This graph addresses some questions about the effect of the Covid-19 pandemic on the unemployment rate.

As we can see, the unemployment rate jumped in 2020 at 8.31%, to a rate not seen since 2011 and in 2021 stood at 8.29%, compared with only 0.02 percent in 2020. Caused by the Covid-19, the unemployment rate has reached a peak in the year of 2020 at 8.31% worldwide - Most people were unable to work because their employer lost or closed their businesses due to the pandemic. It was an unprecedented joblessness amount since employment data collection started in 1948.

This concludes the effect of the Rise of Covid-19 on Unemployment Rate.
